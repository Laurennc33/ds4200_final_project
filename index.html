Exploring Economic Diversity and its Impact on Student Outcomes in U.S Universities

Introduction

The structure of higher education in the United States is heavily defined by its price point, with annual tuition and housing fees at some institutions reaching levels comparable to the average household income. As students at a university with an expected yearly cost of $90,250, we must critically assess the value of such a high-priced education and explore how increasing economic diversity within the student body can affect long-term outcomes. For this project, we sourced the "Economic Diversity and Student Outcomes Data" from Kaggle, which provides insights into economic composition, graduation rates, loan default rates, and post-graduation earnings at U.S. universities. Our goal is to analyze how economic diversity impacts university outcomes and identify institutions that prioritize this aspect. By examining trends and correlations in the data, we aim to better our understanding of educational inequities and spark a conversation about potential solutions to address these disparities.

Introduction to the Data

This dataset was sourced on Kaggle, a public forum for data scientists to share, find, and utilize datasets from across the internet. Users publish datasets they found on the web and can provide information about the usability of the code and other metrics, making it easy to sort through lots of datasets to find one to use without the need for extensive cleaning. This dataset, though found on Kaggle, is sourced from Opportunity Insights, an initiative from Harvard University’s Economics department that aims to use big data to identify barriers to economic opportunity across the United States. The compiled data we found was used in an article by the New York Times to analyze economic segregation in higher education, and takes place in 2024, making it extremely relevant.

Variable Name Details
Rel_Apply Relative application rate, calculated by adjusting score-sending rates to represent the proportion of all standardized test takers that send test scores to given colleges
Par_Income_Bin Parent household income group based on percentile
Tier 'Selective public' 'Highly selective public' 'Other elite schools (public and private)'
Attend_Instate Metric that adjusts attendance rates to reflect proportion of standardized test takers from in state who attend the institution
Attend_Oostate Metric that adjusts attendance rates to reflect the proportion of standardized test takers from out of state who attend the institution.

Along with having an overview of the context, datasets on Kaggle are posted with an in-depth key for the columns of each dataset to make it more understandable. There are over 50 columns in the dataset we selected, but many of them are simply different ways of representing the same variable, or only exist for certain data points and thus will not be utilized in large-scale comparisons. For example, there are separate columns for the relative application rate by SAT score for each school, their standard error, and dependent on school ranking. Continually, variables like the estimation for SAT scores in out of state students are only available in public school data, so they’ll be difficult to compare and normalize and likely won’t be used. One of our potential challenges in this dataset is that there are so many columns, as there will be a bit of work to be done in sorting through them to understand what is relevant to our goals in analyzing and presenting this data.

References

To learn more about how ones socioeconomic status can affect the university they attend, our group referenced two different papers. The first paper we referenced was "The Miseducation of the Eliter" by Jennifer Morton. With the current debate about affirmative action and its effects on the futures of under-represented students, Jennifer M. Morton discusses in her paper The Miseducation of the Elite the high percentage of wealthy, top 1% students in the same elite universities that often funnel into some of the most influential positions in the US and why the diversity of these universities and positions of power need to be examined against how diverse their outcomes are. You can access the paper here The Miseducation of the Elite to explore more of what Jennifer Morton has to say.

The second paper we refernced was "The Price of University: Economic Capital and the Experience of Underrepresented Students in an Elite US University" by Katherine Friend. Why do lower-income students make up a smaller proportion of elite university student bodies? Katherine L. Friend’s book The Price of University: Economic Capital and the Experience of Underrepresented Students in an Elite US University discusses other social and financial constraints to elite university attendance by lower-income students. For example, limited financial aid packages, inaccessible activities, and student loan debt buildup and guilt.
